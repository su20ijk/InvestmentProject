Certificate of Deposit

A Certificate of Deposit is a time deposit, meaning it has a specific maturity date, ranging from a few months all the way up to ten years. CDs are generally issued by banks. CDs can be issued in any denomination above the minimum requirement, which usually is $500 to $1,000. The money needs to remain until the term ends, with penalties for early withdrawals.

CDs are low risk as they are FDIC insured, and most penalities are within control of the depositor.
CDs have fixed interest rates and fixed maturities. CD rates are quoted as annual percentage yields (APY).
CDs are covered by FDIC insurance, as long as its value does not exceed the $250,000 limit.

Pros Cons
  • CDs under $250,000 are FDIC insured
  • CDs have higher interest rates than regular savings accounts
  • Compound interest increases total CD return
  • CDs are not liquid: there are eary withdrawal penalites
  • CD interest rates do not keep up with the rate of inflation

Certificates of Deposit are suited for...


Young to middle aged

Certificates of Deposit are not liquid. The money put into it must be left in it until maturity. You should have access to other money while waiting for the term to end. On top of that is compound interest, which only gets better by time. Younger people should have the time to wait and maximize their return.

Mid to high income

Certificates of Deposit have low interest rates that do not keep up with the inflation rate. Investors should have another source of income as CDs only really supplement someone's earnings. And since the money is inaccessible, investors need to have other sources of income.

Investors looking for low risk and safe investments

Certificates of Deposit are very appealing to the investors looking for safe investments. CDs have low volatility and are insured by the FDIC. These investments are basically guaranteed to pay off, as long as you're willing ot wait for it.
